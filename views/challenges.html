Challenge #1: Geospatial Analysis for Shaping Public Policy (Easy)

Background

It is natural be concerned that flooding will be inundatng our cities as global sea levels continue to rise as the Artic and Antarctic regions continue to melt. As cities take on water there are going to be people with the means to move themselves out of harms way but there are going to be others who lack the means to get out of harms way and may require the coordination of Local, State and Federal officials, developers and landlords (stakeholders) to help rehome displaced people. There are excellent resources available for data that might allow you to advise the stakeholders and steer policy to help those that most need it.

Source 1: Census data: the US census (census.gov) has a ton of variables at the census tract and block group level that can be used to identify areas where the income level is low or high.

Source 2: FEMA – Federal Emergency Management Agency – produces maps that track a handful of natural disasters including coastal and fluvial (river) flooding. These data include fields that score the susceptibility to natural disaster as well as a value for each census tract.

Source 3: Data for Good: be creative with population density data, predicted electrical grids and other sets that could make your analysis interesting.

Source 4: NASA: Global population data lots of options.

Source 5: Elevation data (option). Elevation data will determine the likelihood of flood prone areas within tracts or block groups.

Description

Help shape policy for public and private policy and drive investment to lower risk areas. Requirements include:

  1. Import data from sources formats and use Python, ARCGis or QGis to do geospatial analysis.
  2. Harmonize the data to similar geographic regions. Its easy to get both FEMA data and Census data in the census tract geographic units, harder still, but much more useful is sharing down FEMA data to the block group geographic unit and harmonizing with census data at that level.
  3. Between Census and FEMA data, build a model that looks for lower income neighborhoods with buildings considered to be at much higher risk of flooding.
  4. Once you identify potentially vulnerable populations, look for neighborhoods in metro that are at lower risk of inundation but could accommodate housing. Can you figure out a minimal cost solution for the stakeholders.

Judging Criteria

Business Efficacy:

  1. How will your solution help the stakeholders?
  2. How much money would be saved by your solution than by building in neighboring block groups or tracts?
  3. How effectively can it be used to model other cities?

Accuracy:

  1. Difficulty of harmonizing geography.
  2. Did you find the lower cost solution to the problem.
  3. Thoughtfulness of solution. Are the at risk populations really helped by this solution? are they still in harm’s way.

Scalability:

  1. Can the model adapt to different heights of sea level rise?
  2. Does the model work across multiple markets?
  3. How well can you explain any disparities between the effectiveness of the model between markets?

Storytelling:

  1. How well are you able to explain the effectiveness of your model?
  2. Can you explain how your model works clearly and concisely?
  3. In what ways do you believe your model is actionable?

Challenge #2: Using CNNs to Create Supply Models (Hard)

Background

Supply is one of the most poorly understood aspects of tracking the US industrial real estate market. Often data are not well marked and addresses can be a little inaccurate so CBRE is challenging you to flex your skills and come up with a way to better model the construction process.

Typically an industrial warehouse can be built in anywhere from 2 quarters (6 months) to 6 quarters (18 months) and that range can depend on size is square feet, ceiling height of the building in feet, the loan process, available construction labor, materials costs and availability and weather conditions to name just a few factors. Buildings under 100,000 square feet typically can be built in under 3 quarters, buildings between 200,000 and 500,000 square feet in 4 quarters and 500,000 to 1,000,000 in 5 quarters and greater than a million in 6 quarters. Below we have suppled some construction stages and in the descriptions are the percentage range in the process when we think a project should have reached that stage.

We are going to simplify this change by having you worry only about the data at your disposal and focus on a model that takes into consideration the current stage of construction and its size. When you generate a supply model, you will need to account for how far along the building is in construction and what quarter you believe the building will be in the completed stage.

Description

To build a model for estimating time of construction for Industrial Warehouse in the Atlanta Georgia market. Requirements include:

  1. Import data from the table given in a CSV format.
  2. Acquire recent satellite imagery.
  3. Using a convolutional neural network, attempt to train a model to classify the current point of construction on the points in the CSV file and the satellite imagery.
  4. Given the structure of an estimator, apply that to the data you have tagged with the 4/5 stages shown below to develop a model that predicts the time it will take each building to be completed given how far along the project already is and its size. Make sure, if possible, to record the approximate date of the satellite imagery because adjustments might have to be made to account for the age of the image versus, the stage attained in the image and the current date.
  5. Bonus consideration: are there structures not in the data file but are on a building parcel zoned as industrial that the model picked up off the imagery but are in the stages ground broken, concrete pad or framing going up?

Business Efficacy:

  3. How can it be used by brokerage?
  4. How effectively can it be used to model?

Accuracy:

  2. How accurately did the model classify buildings?
  3. Bonus: can the model pickup buildings in the satellite image that appear in one of the stages: ground broken, concrete pad or framing going up but wasn’t part of the set provided in the CSV file?

Predictability:

  1. Using labelled data, were you able to combine that with the provided market history to create a model of construction out over the next 6 quarters (180 days)?
